Training stability

Z-loss & QK-norm

Two techniques that fix the same root problem — unbounded magnitude growth — in two different places

The shared root problem

Both z-loss and QK-norm solve the same underlying issue from two different angles: unbounded growth during training. As a transformer trains, values at various points in the computation can drift to large magnitudes. When they do, softmax — which appears both in the output layer and inside every attention head — saturates: it peaks sharply toward one option, crushes everything else to near zero, and gradients nearly vanish. Training slows catastrophically or crashes entirely.

Part 1 — Z-loss

The LM head projects the final residual stream to V logits (one per vocabulary token). Softmax converts them to probabilities. Cross-entropy loss is −log(p_correct).

The problem: softmax is invariant to additive shifts but not to scaling. If you scale all logits up together, every token's probability sharpens. During training, nothing in the standard loss prevents logits from growing in absolute magnitude. The model can freely increase confidence on training examples just by scaling logits up — because the correct token's probability rises even if all logits grow proportionally.

Z-loss — three views

The three consequences of logit drift

Numerical overflow. exp(x) overflows float16 for x > ~65,000 and float32 for x > ~88. Logits in the thousands cause silent NaN propagation that crashes training runs.

Softmax saturation. When one logit is much larger than others, it gets probability ≈ 1 and all others ≈ 0. The gradient of softmax is p(1−p) — at p ≈ 1, this is nearly zero. Gradients stop flowing and learning stalls.

Loss spikes. When a saturated model is confidently wrong, the loss is −log(ε) → ∞. One bad batch can blow up a training run.

The fix — z-loss formula

z       = log( Σⱼ exp(logits[j]) )   # log-sum-exp = "partition function"
L_total = L_cross_entropy + ε · z²   # ε ≈ 1e-4 to 1e-5

# Gradient of ε·z² w.r.t. logit j:
∂L_zloss/∂logitⱼ = 2ε · z · softmax(j)
# Pushes all logits down, larger ones pushed harder

Z is the log-partition function — it measures the total mass of the logit distribution. When logits are large and spread out, z is large. Penalising z² pushes the model to keep logits at a moderate absolute scale. The model still learns relative differences between logits (all that matters for predictions), but absolute magnitudes are contained.

ε must be tiny. Values like 1e-4 or 1e-5 mean z-loss barely affects normal training — it only activates as a strong corrective when logits grow unreasonably large. Setting ε too high degrades task performance by over-constraining the logit distribution. Z-loss was introduced and named in the PaLM paper (Google, 2022).

Part 2 — QK-norm

Inside every attention head, the score between token i and token j is:

score(i, j) = Qᵢ · Kⱼ / √d_head

The √d_head denominator was chosen so that when Q and K are unit-variance random vectors at initialisation, scores have variance ≈ 1 — keeping softmax from saturating at the start. The problem: this assumption breaks during training.

Q and K are produced by projecting the residual stream: Q = x·Wq, K = x·Wk. As training progresses the residual stream accumulates contributions from all L layers through residual connections — it grows in norm. Q and K grow with it. The √d_head denominator is fixed, so when Q and K norms double, scores quadruple. When they grow 5×, scores grow 25×. At 80+ layers, this drift can be severe.

QK-norm — three views

The fix — QK-norm formula

# Standard attention (no QK-norm):
Q = x @ Wq                          # [T, d_head] — norm can drift
K = x @ Wk
scores = Q @ K.T / sqrt(d_head)

# With QK-norm:
Q = x @ Wq
K = x @ Wk
Q = RMSNorm(Q) * γ_q                # normalise → unit RMS, then rescale
K = RMSNorm(K) * γ_k                # γ is a learned scalar per head
scores = Q @ K.T / sqrt(d_head)     # now always bounded

RMSNorm divides each vector by its own root-mean-square, forcing ‖Q‖ ≈ √d_head and ‖K‖ ≈ √d_head regardless of what the residual stream is doing. The learned scalar γ (one per head) lets the model control the overall scale of scores through this small controlled parameter — not through unconstrained drift of the entire Q/K space.

Cost: almost nothing. Two RMSNorm operations per layer (very cheap) and 2 × H × L learned scalars (e.g. for a 32-layer, 32-head model: 2 × 32 × 32 = 2,048 extra parameters out of billions). QK-norm is used in Gemma 2, Gemini, and several other recent high-quality models. Particularly valuable at large scale and large depth.
