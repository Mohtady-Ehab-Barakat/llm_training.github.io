Sparsity in LLM training

What a sparse matrix is · FLOPs with and without sparsity · how GPUs exploit it

Most of the compute in an LLM sits inside matrix multiplications — weight matrices multiplied by activations, over and over. If many entries in those matrices are zero, those multiplications are useless (anything × 0 = 0). Sparsity is the idea of identifying and skipping those zeros to do less work.

What does it mean for a matrix to be sparse?

A matrix is sparse when most of its values are exactly zero. The sparsity of a matrix is the fraction of elements that are zero. A 50% sparse matrix has half its entries as zero; a 90% sparse matrix has nine out of every ten entries as zero.

What this means for matmul

Each non-zero element in W requires one multiply and one add when computing W × x. Zero elements produce zero regardless — so on hardware that supports sparsity, those operations are simply skipped.

The FLOP formula

A standard matrix multiply of two matrices with shapes [M, K] and [K, N] produces an [M, N] output. Each of the M × N output elements requires K multiplications and K additions — that's 2K operations per output element, giving us:

FLOPs (dense)  = 2 × M × K × N
FLOPs (sparse) = 2 × M × K × N × (1 − sparsity)

The factor of 2 comes from the multiply-accumulate: for each pair of elements, you do a multiply then an add. At 50% sparsity you do half the multiplications. At 90% sparsity you do only 10%.

Concrete example — one transformer FFN layer: A typical 7B model has FFN weight matrices of shape [4096, 16384]. Dense FLOPs = 2 × 4096 × 16384 ≈ 134M FLOPs. At 50% sparsity that drops to ~67M FLOPs per forward pass through that layer.

Why sparsity is tricky on a GPU

A GPU is built to run millions of multiply-adds in parallel. The problem is that naive sparse computation is actually slower on a GPU — skipping random zeros means jumping around memory unpredictably, which destroys the cache efficiency that makes GPUs fast in the first place.

The solution is structured sparsity. NVIDIA's A100 introduced hardware support for 2:4 sparsity — a constraint where exactly 2 of every 4 consecutive weights must be zero. This fixed pattern is regular enough that dedicated sparse tensor cores can skip the zeros without any irregular memory access, delivering a genuine 2× throughput on those operations.

The practical caveat: 50% sparsity doesn't give you 2× wall-clock speedup. You still pay for storing the sparse format (indices of non-zeros), the overhead of pruning and potentially fine-tuning, and not all operations benefit equally. Real-world speedups on well-optimised hardware are typically 1.3–1.7×.

Where sparsity shows up in LLM training

Weight pruning
After initial training, small weights are zeroed out ("pruned"), then the model is fine-tuned to recover accuracy. This is the most common form.
Activation sparsity
ReLU produces exact zeros for negative inputs. SwiGLU (used in LLaMA etc.) produces near-zeros that can be thresholded. Up to 90%+ of FFN activations can be zero.
Gradient sparsity
Gradients during backprop are often very sparse, especially early in training. Gradient compression methods exploit this to reduce communication in multi-GPU training.
Attention sparsity
Most attention weights are near-zero for distant tokens. Sparse attention patterns (local + global) like those in Longformer reduce the O(n²) cost of full attention.
2:4 structured
NVIDIA's hardware-enforced pattern: 2 non-zeros per group of 4. Supported natively on A100/H100 sparse tensor cores with 2× throughput.
