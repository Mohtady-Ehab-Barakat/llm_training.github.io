Floating Point Formats

float32 · float16 · bfloat16 — what the bits actually do

Every number in a neural network is stored as a floating point value. The format determines two things: how large a number can be (dynamic range, controlled by exponent bits) and how precise it can be (controlled by fraction bits).

Bit layout

Each format allocates its 16 or 32 bits differently between three fields:

The bfloat16 trade-off: it keeps float32's 8 exponent bits (same dynamic range) but shrinks the fraction from 23 → 7 bits (lower precision). float16 went the opposite way: 5 exponent bits → tiny range, 10 fraction bits → more precision per number.

Why this matters in ML training

During backpropagation, gradients can become extremely small — especially in deep networks. If you store them in float16, small gradients silently underflow to zero and training breaks. bfloat16 avoids this by keeping the full float32 exponent range.

For ML training, range matters more than precision. bfloat16 is the preferred format on TPUs and modern GPUs (A100, H100) because it gives float32 range at half the memory. float16 often needs loss scaling tricks to compensate for its narrow range.
float32
Default. Full precision. Safe for everything but uses most memory.
float16
Half memory, decent precision, but gradient underflow risk. Needs loss scaling.
bfloat16
Half memory, same range as float32, lower precision. Best for training on modern hardware.
